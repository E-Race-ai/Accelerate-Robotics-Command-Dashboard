In-Residence Dining Workflow — Torno Subito × S100

The S100 is a transport platform — it moves the meal between two humans. The choreography is designed so Torno's pass-side plating standards, health-code compliance, and Michelin Key service expectations are never compromised by the robot.

1

Member orders. In-residence tablet, phone, or Moore app → Torno POS (Toast or Simphony).

2

Kitchen plates. Torno's runner plates on porcelain at the pass — same as today.

3

Load thermal modules. Hot tier: stainless cloche (140°F+). Cold: phase-change cradle (40°F−). Runner taps "dispatch."

4

Dispatch. S100 rides elevator to residence. 4–6 min pass → door. Temp logged per order.

5

Member notified. Residence-phone call via PBX + gentle knock-tone. Touchscreen auth unlocks top lid.

6

Tray removal. Top tier slides out as a single tray. Robot waits 90 sec for additional tiers.

7

Pickup return. Scheduled sweep (every 45 min) or member-triggered. Runner reloads for dish-room.

What This Preserves

  • ✓ Torno plates, loads, retrieves. Robot replaces the elevator-and-corridor walk — the lowest-value leg of the trip.
  • ✓ Food-safety compliance. Temperature logged per order; thermal modules NSF-certified; phase-change packs cycle through Torno's walk-in on banquet-caddy schedule.
  • ✓ Plating standards. Top tier is a removable tray the member sees open — looks like residence dining service, not "robot output." Bronze/oak/leather shell is cosmetic; thermal modules work invisibly.

What This Does NOT Claim

  • ✕ Not a replacement for in-residence dining attendants on premium orders (tasting menus, wine service, table-side).
  • ✕ Not a carrier for items requiring active heating — no ovens on board.
  • ✕ Not a substitute for Torno runners during peak dinner service. Humans handle complex orders; S100 handles simple parallel runs (post-service late-night, breakfast, amenity drops).

Elevator Operations

The Moore operates 2 Otis elevators serving floors 1–4. Robot-elevator coexistence with a 13-residence club is a design constraint we've built the operating plan around.

Member-Yield Protocol

Robots always yield to waiting members. If a member is waiting on any floor, the robot holds position and takes the next available car. Members never wait for a robot.

Off-Peak Scheduling

Cleaning robots operate 2 AM–6 AM when elevator demand is minimal. Full floor sequences before returning to dock — minimizing total trips through the shared stack.

Batch Transport

Linen and event-turnover robots batch their loads — one full cart per trip instead of multiple partial runs. Fewer elevator trips, more efficient logistics.

Non-Invasive Integration

Our universal button emulator doesn't modify Otis firmware or require an OEM API contract. Fully reversible hardware layer — leaves the elevator as we found it if removed.

Realistic impact at peak fleet (Option C): 5–8 additional elevator trips/hour during overnight cleaning; 2–4 trips/hour during daytime (on-demand member delivery). Two elevators serving four floors with 13 residences handle well under capacity in member windows. Robot traffic is a modest incremental load concentrated outside peak member hours.

Otis Contingency

The button emulator is non-invasive, but physical installation still requires Otis review. We take that risk rather than pushing it to The Moore.

Surface 2

Financial Operations Dashboard

Oracle MICROS Simphony / RES 3700 overhaul · fleet management integrated.

A full overhaul of the club's POS reporting stack, with the robot fleet treated as a first-class line item. Three reports anchor the surface — the MICROS Manager's Report, the CFO Close-Out Report, and the Fleet Performance Report — all living inside one dashboard and all built around the questions a GM, CFO, and operations lead actually ask.

The problem this solves

Native MICROS reports bury the issues that matter. A recent daily summary from a comparable F&B operation immediately surfaced six control issues that had been running invisibly: a -$3,273 negative cash position, a $4,161 cash-due vs. deposit variance, $688 of missing revenue, COGS and labor cost not configured, unauthorized comps across servers, and a dead revenue center still in the active roll-up. Standard MICROS reports surface none of this on a single pane — and they have no concept of the fleet at all.

The Physical Infrastructure

BLE Ceiling Anchor

48 UNITS

UWB Precision Anchor

7 UNITS

Wall/corner mount at chokepoints (elevators, Torno pass, event-room doors). Sub-30cm accuracy.

BLE Asset Tag

65 UNITS

Adhesive or clip-on. Attached to service carts, linen totes, event-turnover kits, housekeeping caddies.

How It Works

1

Tag Attached

An asset tag is affixed to a service cart, linen tote, or event-turnover kit. Each tag broadcasts a unique ID every few seconds.

2

Anchors Listen

Ceiling + wall anchors pick up the signal. Three or more anchors triangulate the tag's position in real time.

3

Dashboard Updates

Position data streams to the facility dashboard. Staff see every tagged asset on a live floor map — where it is, how long it's been idle.

4

Robot Dispatched

Rules trigger action — a linen cart idle >20 min in a corridor? The nearest robot is dispatched to retrieve it. No ticket, no radio call.

Same infrastructure; different use cases. Once the anchors are live, new workflows are software configuration — not new hardware.
