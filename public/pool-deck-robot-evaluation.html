Thesis Hotel Pool Deck — Slip-Hazard Mitigation & Wet Cleaning Market Scan, Q2 2026

Working Draft Confidential Working Draft

The Thesis Hotel pool deck is a textured concrete surround with continuous standing-water slip hazards, pool-edge fall risk, chlorine exposure, and daily debris accumulation. This document is a full market scan of every robot that could plausibly clean the deck surface — commercial wet scrubbers with squeegee recovery, 4-in-1 sweep/scrub/vacuum/mop robots, outdoor sweepers, in-pool cleaners (for comparison), and consumer wet-dry hybrids — with an honest market-gap assessment at the end.

Headline finding: There is currently no robot on the market purpose-built for outdoor textured pool-deck wet cleaning with standing-water recovery. Every viable candidate is an indoor commercial scrubber adapted to a semi-outdoor use, or an outdoor sweeper that only handles dry debris. The recommendation reflects this gap and proposes a pragmatic dual-deployment plus a product R&D flag for Accelerate Robotics' own platform roadmap.

Every robot in this evaluation was verified against manufacturer spec sheets and current 2026 availability. Pricing is indicative only; quote every candidate before committing.

1. The Use Case

The Space

The Thesis Hotel pool deck is a Florida resort-grade pool surround: a curved pool body with a waterfall feature, flagstone coping, a textured concrete deck in a knockdown ("Kool-Deck" style) finish, and a tree canopy above. It is a continuously guest-facing environment where slip-and-fall liability and visual polish matter as much as cleaning performance.

Surface: Textured "knockdown" concrete (Kool-Deck style)
Approximate area: 500–1,200 m² (est., pool surround)
Pool edge: Flagstone coping, no raised curb
Water features: Pool + rock waterfall (continuous overspray)
Overhead: Tree canopy (leaf fall year-round)
Access path: Gated pool area, guest traffic continuous daytime

The Hazards

This is fundamentally a safety problem, not a cosmetic one. A pool deck is the single highest slip-hazard surface in a hotel. The cleaning robot's primary job is to reduce the standing-water dwell time on the deck, not just to look tidy.

  • Standing water — splash from the pool, waterfall overspray, swimmer runoff, rain. Accumulates in the pockets of the knockdown texture and does not evaporate quickly in humid Florida air.
  • Organic slick — sunscreen residue, pool-chemistry foam, algae on shaded areas of the deck
  • Leaf and petal debris from the overhanging trees (mostly dry, but sticks to wet surfaces)
  • Pool chemistry splash — chlorinated or salt water on robot chassis and internals
  • Sand and grit tracked in from walking paths; wears squeegees and brushes fast

The Surface

The textured surface is the single biggest complication in this scan. A knockdown-finish pool deck has shallow pockets and ridges specifically engineered to provide barefoot grip when wet — exactly the feature that defeats a flat rubber squeegee blade. Water recovery on this surface is a hard engineering problem, not a configuration problem.

Texture limitation: A standard rubber squeegee rides over the high points of a knockdown texture and cannot draw water out of the low points. This means any robot with a flat recovery squeegee will leave 10–30% residual standing water in the texture pockets. The only technology family that addresses this directly is ECOVACS Commercial's "water absorption" multi-port dispense-and-vacuum head. See Candidate 1.

The Climate

Summer high: 95°F / 35°C
Humidity: 75–95% year-round
Rain: Daily afternoon showers, June–Oct
Sun exposure: Direct UV, 8+ hours most days
Chemistry exposure: Chlorine aerosol, possible salt-chlorination
Wind: Gulf storms, hurricane season

No-Go Zones

  • Pool body itself (falls risk — any robot must stop well clear of the coping)
  • Waterfall feature and rock cascade
  • Any raised flagstone edge where a drive wheel could catch
  • Guest-seating areas when occupied
  • Chaise lounges, umbrellas, towel bins

1. ECOVACS DEEBOT PRO M1 Top Pick (Slip-Hazard)

Honest Finding: The Market Has a Gap

No robot on the market today was built for outdoor textured pool-deck wet cleaning with standing-water recovery. Every wet robot in this scan is indoor-targeted. Every outdoor robot is dry-only. The best available answer is an indoor commercial scrubber deployed semi-outdoors under a written outdoor-use agreement, paired with an outdoor-rated dry sweeper for the debris half of the job.

Primary: ECOVACS DEEBOT PRO M1 (Wet / Slip Hazard)

The M1 is the only robot in the scan whose core engineering directly addresses the slip-hazard problem. Its multi-port water dispense + integrated vacuum ("Water Absorption Technology") is the closest any vendor gets to recovering water out of the pockets of a knockdown-texture concrete surface, rather than riding over them with a flat squeegee. Triple-sealed motor and anti-corrosion impeller give it the best chance of tolerating chlorine aerosol of any candidate in the scan.

The M1's weakness is that ECOVACS does not publish an outdoor IP rating. This must be negotiated at the pilot stage: a written statement from the distributor confirming the M1 is covered for outdoor deck use at Thesis, or a loaner agreement structured so the unit can be returned if Florida humidity and UV cause a problem.

Secondary: PUDU CC1 (Wet / Pragmatic Fallback)

If the ECOVACS distributor will not commit to an outdoor-use statement, the PUDU CC1 is the next best wet robot. Same 4-in-1 mode set, auto water refill/drain docking, ~$917/mo RaaS. It uses a conventional squeegee + vacuum recovery so it will leave more residual water in the deck's texture pockets than the M1, but it is a known commercial-grade machine with established US service.

Paired Dry Partner: PUDU MT1 Max

Whichever wet robot is chosen, pair it with the PUDU MT1 Max from the outdoor courtyard evaluation. The MT1 Max is the only genuinely outdoor-rated robot in the scan — IP54, rain sensor, dual-disc brushes, 35 L bin — and it handles leaf and petal debris under the tree canopy natively. A dual-robot deployment is the clean answer: the MT1 Max covers the dry work (leaves, sand, grit), and the ECOVACS M1 or PUDU CC1 covers the wet work (standing water, sunscreen slick, pool splash).

R&D Flag for Accelerate Robotics Platform Roadmap

Platform opportunity: This market gap is a first-party product opportunity for Accelerate Robotics, not just a procurement exercise. An outdoor-rated, chlorine-tolerant, texture-adaptive wet cleaning robot for hospitality pool decks does not exist today. A first-party reference design — or a co-development partnership with ECOVACS or PUDU — could become the second "wedge" product after the elevator integration layer. Worth tracking as a Phase 5 roadmap item and flagging to advisors at FON Advisory as part of the government/GSA hospitality opportunity set.

7. Pilot Path

This is the recommended 4-week sequence to evaluate and deploy a pool-deck wet robot at the Thesis Hotel. It mirrors the outdoor courtyard playbook so the hotel GM sees a consistent process across zones.

Week 1 — Site Survey & Baseline

  • On-site walk with ECOVACS Commercial and PUDU distributor representatives
  • Measure deck dimensions, flagstone coping geometry, chaise lounge layout, shade patterns
  • Baseline wet test: pour measured water onto deck, time natural evaporation by zone (shade vs. sun), photograph residual water in texture pockets
  • Confirm pool chemistry (chlorine vs. salt chlorination) and daily aerosol exposure pattern
  • Identify dock location under cover / shade

Week 2 — Loaner Evaluation (Wet)

  • 7-day on-site loaner: ECOVACS DEEBOT PRO M1 (or PUDU CC1 if M1 unavailable)
  • Run morning and late-afternoon cycles
  • Primary KPI: residual water measured at standard grid points 5 minutes after a pass (slip-hazard proxy)
  • Validate pool-edge detection on flagstone coping
  • Stress test: sunscreen spill, leaf slick, chlorine splash zone near waterfall
  • Document chassis condition daily — photograph any corrosion, discoloration, or squeegee wear

Week 3 — Dry Partner Loaner & Dual-Robot Integration

  • Overlap week with the PUDU MT1 Max loaner from the courtyard evaluation
  • Run both robots on the deck in sequence: MT1 Max dry pass first, wet robot second
  • Confirm the two robots can coexist in the same Accelerate fleet management view
  • Collect combined cleanliness + residual-water score for the dual-deployment

Week 4 — Commercial Proposal & Commissioning

  • Present dual-robot RaaS pricing to Thesis Hotel GM (bundled with existing indoor fleet + courtyard outdoor fleet)
  • Draft Phase 5 addendum to the existing proposal document — pool deck slip-hazard line item
  • Negotiate outdoor-use warranty statement from ECOVACS distributor (the critical contractual step)
  • Ship production units, 3-day on-site commissioning, staff training, go-live

9. Conclusion

Twelve robots looked at; zero were purpose-built for this job. The ECOVACS DEEBOT PRO M1 is the closest available match because its water-absorption head is the only technology in the market that directly prevents residual water on a textured deck surface. Everything else is indoor, dry-only, wrong-category, or wrong-scale. The honest recommendation is a dual-robot deployment — one wet, one dry — plus an R&D flag for Accelerate Robotics to consider building a first-party answer to this gap.

The recommended path is (1) move to a site survey with ECOVACS Commercial and PUDU distributors, (2) run a 7-day wet loaner with residual-water measurement as the pass/fail KPI, (3) overlap with the PUDU MT1 Max loaner from the outdoor courtyard evaluation for a combined dual-deployment demo, and (4) propose a Phase 5 addendum to the Thesis Hotel proposal that bundles wet + dry robot service into a single pool-deck slip-hazard line item.

If no robot in the current market can meet the residual-water KPI on the knockdown deck, Accelerate Robotics should treat that finding as a product roadmap signal, not a dead end. The first hotel to solve the pool-deck slip-hazard problem with robotics wins a category that currently does not have a vendor.
